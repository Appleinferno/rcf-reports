How to Prepare to Install Floating Hardwood

Every successful flooring installation begins long before the first plank is laid. Prep work is what separates a floor that lasts 30 years from one that develops gaps and squeaks in the first year.

Here's what to do before you start clicking planks together:

  1. Acclimate your flooring - Stack the boxes in the room where they'll be installed for at least 48 to 72 hours. Engineered hardwood adjusts to the ambient temperature and humidity of the space.
  2. Check your subfloor - It needs to be flat to within 3/16" per 10 feet. High spots can cause clicking issues. Low spots can create hollow sounds underfoot.
  3. Install underlayment - Unless your product has pre-attached padding, roll out a quality foam or cork underlayment first. This handles moisture and sound.
  4. Leave expansion gaps - Engineered hardwood still moves slightly with seasonal humidity changes. Leave at least 1/4" to 3/8" around all walls and fixed objects.
  5. Lay out your plan - Start from the longest, straightest wall. Map out your rows so you don't end up with a tiny sliver of plank at the finish wall.

Common Mistakes to Avoid When Installing A Floating Floor

The install is simple. But simple doesn't mean there's no room to go wrong. Avoid these common mistakes and your project stays on track.

  • Skipping acclimation - Rushing this step causes gapping and buckling later. Give the wood time to settle into your home's environment.
  • Ignoring moisture - Floating floors over concrete without a proper moisture barrier can cause serious damage. Test your slab before you start.
  • Forgetting matching trim - Order your matching trim pieces at the same time you order your flooring. Waiting until install day to order transitions creates costly delays.
  • Running planks the wrong direction - In most rooms, planks should run parallel to the longest wall. In narrow hallways, run them lengthwise. Direction affects how the floor reads visually and structurally.
  • Under-ordering - Always add 10% for cuts and waste on a standard rectangular room. Add 15% if you have lots of angles, doorways, or staggered layouts.
